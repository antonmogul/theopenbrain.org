# Appendix: Key Terms Glossary

## Visual Anatomy

**Fovea**: The small pit in the center of the retina containing the highest density of cone photoreceptors. Responsible for sharp central vision, spanning approximately 2 degrees of visual angle. Only the fovea provides high-acuity vision sufficient for reading and detailed recognition.

**Parafovea**: The retinal region extending 2-5 degrees from the fovea. Provides lower-resolution vision than the fovea but contributes to reading through preview of upcoming words during fixations.

**Peripheral vision**: Vision outside the central 5 degrees. Characterized by low spatial acuity, strong motion detection, and limited color perception. Covers most of the visual field but cannot resolve fine detail.

**V1 (Primary Visual Cortex)**: The first cortical area to process visual information, located at the back of the brain. Contains neurons that detect oriented edges and simple visual features. Also called striate cortex.

**V5/MT (Mediotemporal Area)**: A specialized visual cortical area responsible for motion perception. Damage to this area causes motion blindness (akinetopsia) while leaving other visual functions intact.

**LGN (Lateral Geniculate Nucleus)**: A relay station in the thalamus where visual information passes from the retina to the cortex. Receives feedback from cortex, suggesting it actively filters visual information based on attention and expectation.

**Ventral stream**: The "what" pathway—a visual processing route from V1 to temporal lobe that handles object recognition, face identification, and reading.

**Dorsal stream**: The "where" pathway—a visual processing route from V1 to parietal lobe that handles spatial relationships, motion processing, and visually guided action.

## Attention

**Selective attention**: The cognitive process of focusing on relevant information while filtering out irrelevant stimuli. Allows processing of limited information from the vast sensory input reaching the brain.

**Inattentional blindness**: The failure to notice fully visible objects or events when attention is engaged elsewhere. Demonstrates that visibility does not guarantee awareness—attention is required for conscious perception.

**Change blindness**: The failure to detect changes in visual scenes, particularly when changes occur during visual disruption (blinks, saccades, cuts). Reveals that we maintain less detailed visual representation than intuition suggests.

**Attentional blink**: A temporary impairment in detecting a second target appearing 200-500ms after detecting a first target. Results from a consolidation bottleneck in working memory.

**Saccade**: A rapid, ballistic eye movement used to shift gaze from one fixation point to another. Typically lasts 20-200ms and occurs 3-4 times per second during active viewing.

**Fixation**: A pause in eye movement during which visual information is processed. Typically lasts 200-300ms during reading, longer during complex processing.

**Saccadic suppression**: The active suppression of visual processing during saccades, which prevents perception of blur or motion during eye movements.

**Banner blindness**: The learned tendency to ignore page regions associated with advertising, regardless of actual content. Results from selective attention filtering based on spatial location and visual characteristics.

## Gestalt Principles

**Proximity**: The grouping principle that elements close together are perceived as belonging to the same group. One of the most powerful tools for communicating information relationships in interfaces.

**Similarity**: The grouping principle that elements sharing visual features (color, shape, size, orientation) are perceived as related, even when spatially separated.

**Closure**: The perceptual tendency to complete incomplete forms, perceiving complete shapes from fragmentary information. Enables effective use of negative space in design.

**Continuity (Good Continuation)**: The perceptual tendency to perceive smooth, continuous paths rather than abrupt directional changes. Alignment creates visual flow that guides attention.

**Common fate**: The grouping principle that elements moving together in the same direction are perceived as belonging together. Foundation for using animation to communicate relationships.

**Figure-ground segregation**: The fundamental perceptual process of separating objects (figures) from their backgrounds. Essential for interface design where interactive elements must be perceived as distinct from surrounding content.

## Color Perception

**Trichromacy**: The basis of human color vision in three cone types (S, M, L) with different wavelength sensitivities. All perceived colors result from combinations of activation across these three channels.

**Opponent process**: The neural coding of color into three opponent channels: light-dark, red-green, and blue-yellow. Explains why you cannot perceive "reddish-green" or "yellowish-blue."

**Color constancy**: The perceptual ability to perceive consistent object colors despite varying illumination. Involves complex computations that estimate and discount illumination effects.

**Color deficiency (color blindness)**: Reduced ability to discriminate colors due to absent or altered cone types. Most commonly affects red-green discrimination (approximately 8% of males).

**Contrast ratio**: A measure of luminance difference between foreground and background, calculated as the ratio of lighter to darker luminance. WCAG specifies minimum ratios for accessibility.

**Simultaneous contrast**: The phenomenon where surrounding colors influence perceived color of a target. The same gray appears warmer on a blue background and cooler on an orange background.

## Cognitive

**Working memory**: The cognitive system responsible for temporary storage and manipulation of information. Has limited capacity (approximately 4 chunks) and requires attention to maintain.

**Cognitive load**: The mental effort required to process information, make decisions, and execute actions. Cognitive load theory distinguishes intrinsic, extraneous, and germane load.

**Chunking**: The process of organizing individual items into larger meaningful units, effectively increasing working memory capacity. Expertise enables more effective chunking.

**Progressive disclosure**: A design pattern of revealing information and options in layers, presenting most frequently needed functionality first and revealing complexity as users need it.

**Schema**: Organized knowledge structures in long-term memory that guide perception and comprehension. Experts possess rich schemas that reduce cognitive load in their domains.

**Miller's Law**: The observation that working memory capacity is approximately 7±2 chunks. Modern research suggests the base capacity may be closer to 4±1 items without chunking.

## Motion and Time Perception

**Apparent motion (Phi phenomenon)**: The perception of motion from rapid alternation of static stimuli. The foundation of all screen-based motion, including video and animation.

**Motion aftereffect**: An illusion where stationary objects appear to move in the opposite direction after prolonged viewing of motion (the "waterfall illusion").

**Biological motion**: The characteristic movement patterns of living things. Humans have specialized sensitivity to biological motion, able to recognize it from minimal cues.

**Perceived duration**: Subjective experience of time passing, which differs from objective clock time based on factors including uncertainty, engagement, progress, and anxiety.

**prefers-reduced-motion**: A CSS media query that detects when users have requested reduced motion in their system preferences, enabling design of reduced-motion alternatives.

## Bias and Decision-Making

**Anchoring**: The cognitive bias of over-relying on the first piece of information encountered when making judgments. Even arbitrary anchors influence subsequent estimates.

**Loss aversion**: The psychological phenomenon where losses feel approximately twice as painful as equivalent gains feel pleasurable. Foundation for framing effects.

**Framing effect**: How information presentation affects decisions independent of underlying content. The same information framed as gain vs. loss produces different choices.

**Default bias**: The strong tendency to accept default options rather than making active choices. Defaults have disproportionate influence on outcomes.

**Decoy effect (Asymmetric dominance)**: The phenomenon where adding a dominated option increases preference for the dominating option. Used extensively in pricing strategies.

**Nudge**: A choice architecture intervention that influences behavior without restricting options. Examples include defaults, framing, and social proof.

**Choice architecture**: The practice of designing contexts in which people make choices, recognizing that presentation inevitably influences decisions.
